Research Interest Summary

Computer Science Theory Education

Anecdotally, I have noticed many of my peers hesitate when I recommend that they take upper-division courses in computer science theory, with many of them questioning their ability to succeed. Some of them don't know whether they're creative enough to design algorithms, while others are concerned about the mathematical rigor necessary to analyze algorithms. These experiences have motivated my interests in discovering and alleviating barriers to success in computer science theory courses. I'm curious about the factors that influence sense of belonging, self-efficacy, and imposter syndrome in computer science theory. Ultimately, my goal is to use qualitative methods to understand student experiences so that we can identify why some students are hesitant to dive deeper into computer science theory or are doubtful of their ability to grasp computer science theory concepts. My hope is that work in this field helps guide the design of various interventions to support student success in computer science theory courses.


Algorithm Design

While I am broadly interested in computer science theory as a whole, I have taken a particular interest to designing algorithms that not only achieve good theoretical bounds but can also be implemented in practice. This includes verifying our algorithms' theoretical bounds and comparing them against state-of-the-art algorithms via experimental analysis. With regard to algorithm design techniques, I am primarily intrigued by reductions and the application of subroutines as building blocks for a larger algorithm. For example, I am fascinated by the applications of Grover's algorithm to achieve speedups in hybrid quantum-classical algorithms and the applications of prefix sum in parallel algorithm design. Formulating problems in ways that make existing subroutines useful opens up new ways to design efficient algorithms while simultaneously simplifying the analysis.
